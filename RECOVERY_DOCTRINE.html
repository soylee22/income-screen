Where the income screen rejects a fallen stock as a falling knife, this one asks whether the knife is mispriced.

01

The thesis

"What could this company earn under normal conditions and normal management?" Not what it earns today, depressed by the cycle or a fixable problem.

If a sound business is temporarily earning far below its mid-cycle margin, the market often prices it on today's bad number, sometimes at 3 to 4 times normalized earnings. When earnings recover and the multiple re-rates from distressed to a normal ~12 times, you are paid twice: the earnings normalise and the rating expands. That is the 3-to-4-times-your-money mechanic of recovery value (Beagles / Temple Bar school).

upside = normalized earnings × 12 ÷ price − 1   (recovery + re-rating)

Normalized earnings here = the winsorised median operating margin over the available years (median, so one-off disposal-gain years do not distort it) applied to current revenue, taxed. A company whose current margin sits well below that normal level is the candidate.

02

The mirror image of the income screen

Why two tools

This scanner buys exactly what the income screen throws away

The income screen has a falling-knife gate: it drops any name down more than 10% over a year, because for a buy-and-hold income compounder a crashing stock is usually bad news. Recovery value makes the opposite bet, the depressed, fallen, out-of-favour names are where the 3-to-4-times-normalized multiples live. So the income screen's rejects are this tool's candidates. Same data, opposite stance. They are different strategies for different money: one is hold-forever income, the other is buy-cheap, sell at the re-rating in roughly five years.

03

The criteria

The whole risk is buying a value trap (cheap on "normalized" earnings that never return) rather than a recovery. The gates separate "average business that can get better" from "broken":

  1. Operating company, market cap ≥ $5bn (financials excluded for now, they need price-to-book on normalized ROE, a different model)
  2. Was a real business: normalized operating margin ≥ 5% (it earns proper money in a normal year)
  3. Currently depressed: current margin below 85% of normalized (the recovery has not happened yet, so the upside is still there)
  4. Survivable: net debt / EBITDA ≤ 5 and the gross margin (the franchise) still intact, so it lives long enough to recover
  5. Cheap on normalized: normalized P/E ≤ 12, and not in active free-fall (not down >60% in a year, which usually means genuinely broken)

Survivors are ranked by implied upside: normalized earnings at a 12× multiple versus today's price.

04

What it cannot do

Honest limits

Thin data, and the recovery may never come

Two real caveats. First, the normalization is only as good as the history: Yahoo gives roughly four years of income statements, not the seven-to-ten a proper through-cycle average wants, so a company depressed for all four years can look "normal" at its depressed level. Second, and fundamentally, this strategy is right only when the earnings actually recover. A genuine structural decline (not a cycle) is a value trap that stays cheap forever. The gates filter for survivability and an intact franchise, but they cannot tell a cyclical dip from a terminal one, that is a forward judgement on the business, which stays with you. The scanner finds the candidates; it does not promise the recovery.
