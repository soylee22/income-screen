Equity income · Acquisition engine

Acquire income on evidence, not on yield.

A selection engine for a buy-and-hold dividend snowball. It assembles, over years of monthly purchases, a growing and durable income stream without overpaying at the point of entry. It does not chase yield, time charts, or promise alpha. It generates a ranked candidate list and imposes the one discipline the evidence rewards.

01

The mandate

This is not a "beat the market" engine. The objective is a growing, durable stream of dividend income, accumulated through monthly buys, without overpaying at entry. Total return is a constraint (do not destroy capital), not the goal.

The single decision it informs is narrow and concrete: "I have a fixed amount this month. Which one name do I buy?" Everything below serves that question. The screen produces a ranked candidate list, not a portfolio, and makes no promise of alpha. Its real work is twofold: stop us buying yield traps, and enforce the monthly buying habit that the evidence says matters more than any clever entry.

02

The universe

~{{NUNIV}} large caps: FTSE 100 + S&P 500 + CAC 40 + DAX, plus Swiss and Hong Kong majors. Large-cap only, deliberately. Every piece of evidence that supports this approach (the quality premium, dividend-grower outperformance) lives in large, liquid names; every timing anomaly that fails lives in small, illiquid ones. Constituents are scraped from public index lists and date-stamped, so the universe rebuilds itself as indices reconstitute.

Key finding

Almost no UK survives, and that is the screen working

Of 100 FTSE 100 names, five pass. The UK large-cap market is bifurcated: low-yield quality-growth (AstraZeneca 1.7%, RELX 2.6%, Rolls 0.8%) that fails the 3% income floor, and high-yield-but-cut-prone (all five big banks suspended in 2020 under PRA instruction; Shell and BP cut in 2020; miners, telecoms and utilities pay variable dividends) that fails the unbroken-record gate. The quality-and-sustained-and-yielding middle is genuinely thin in the UK. IUKD, the pure UK high-yield index, is the textbook long-run underperformer for exactly this reason.

Implication: this is a global quality-income screen. UK exposure should come from your index core, not from this sleeve.

One deliberate exception: a documented PRA-override list restores the big UK banks and insurers (HSBC, NatWest, Lloyds, Aviva) whose only blemish was the 2020 dividend suspension that the Bank of England's PRA forced on the whole sector, and which each has since restored to above 90% of its 2019 level. Those names carry a PRA tag in the table. They still clear every other gate. Several rank low on cheapness precisely because they have since rallied hard (HSBC up ~48%), which is the screen working: included, but not flagged as cheap.

03

The criteria

A name must clear every gate. Survivors are then scored 0 to 1 on a weighted composite.

Gates · binary, all required

  1. Size at least $15bn market cap (FX-converted). BDCs use a $2bn floor (the sector is structurally smaller)
  2. Yield between 3% and 8% (BDCs up to 12%, their normal band)
  3. Record: 10+ years paying, no >20% annual cut in the last 10 (2020/21 COVID and PRA cuts forgiven if recovered to 90% of 2019)
  4. Quality, by lane (one metric is not right for every asset): operating companies on ROIC ≥ 8% or ROA ≥ 5% (robust to the buyback-driven equity distortion that breaks ROE); banks and insurers on ROE ≥ 8%; REITs on FFO dividend coverage ≥ 1.05× (Funds From Operations = net income + property depreciation; ROE is a category error for REITs because depreciation crushes GAAP earnings while the buildings hold value, the reason Realty Income's ROE is ~3% yet its dividend is amply covered). MAIN, the one BDC, uses ROE (which IS meaningful for a leveraged loan book) with its price/NAV premium shown
  5. Cash cover (non-financials): free cash flow at least 1.3× dividends, from the audited cash-flow statement. Utilities are judged on operating cash flow ≥ 1.5× dividends instead: FCF-after-growth-capex is the wrong metric for a rate-base builder, which funds the dividend from OCF and finances the build separately (the same coverage logic as REIT FFO)
  6. Leverage (non-financials): net debt (debt minus cash) / EBITDA at most 4, relaxed to 6.5 for regulated utilities (contracted/rate-regulated cash flows safely support more leverage). ROIC is also waived for utilities, depressed as it is by the vast regulated asset base; OCF cover + the leverage ceiling + the 10-year uncut record are the guardrail. Admits the utility aristocrats (ConEd, Southern, Duke, Fortis) while real cutters (Dominion, PG&E) and the genuinely over-levered (National Grid, DTE) are still cut
  7. Trend: not in a clear downtrend, 12-month return better than minus 10% and not more than 12% below its 200-day average (the falling-knife filter)
  8. Earnings not in freefall (operating companies): net income has not fallen more than 10%/yr over the cycle. Profit, not revenue, so flat-revenue cash machines with pricing power (tobacco, staples) are spared while genuine deterioration (patent cliffs, margin collapses) is cut

Rank · expected return (Gordon / DDM)

Survivors are ranked by an estimate of expected total return, in real percentage points, so the number is interpretable and includes the income level itself:

exp return = net yield + sustainable growth + reversion

  • Net yield: after-withholding-tax current yield (the income, which a pure factor blend leaves out)
  • Sustainable growth: robust 8-year dividend-growth slope blended with a forward estimate, capped at 8% (long-run real growth is ~1.8%/yr, so high growth must not be trusted at face value), haircut by ROIC quality and by the raise streak (below)
  • Reversion: a modest cross-sectional cheapness tilt (FCF yield vs the other candidates), the evidence-backed form of value

The raise streak (Strk column) is consecutive annual dividend increases, bridged across the 2020/21 PRA-forced cuts. It does not add return; it weights how much of the growth we believe. A dividend raise is a costly, forward-looking signal (Bhattacharya 1979; Miller-Rock 1985): a board only raises when it judges the cash flow durable. So a high growth figure off a short streak is rebound-from-a-cut and is discounted, while a clean long-streak raiser keeps full growth credit. This is empirically near-orthogonal to yield, years and growth in our universe, so it adds information rather than double-counting. It is a credibility multiplier, never an additive bonus, so the expected-return number stays honest.

The estimate is deliberately forward-looking. A 2008-crisis dividend-fragility penalty was trialled and removed: it was 18 years old under a since-changed bank-capital regime, redundant with the recent 2020 stress already in the gates, and the European 2008 dividend data was too unreliable to score fairly. The financials concentration it was meant to fix is handled at allocation by the sector cap, not by baking an old event into the number.

This is the theory of dividend returns, not an arbitrary weighting. It dissolves the old yield-vs-growth argument: a high yielder and a fast grower are scored on the same expected-return basis and the maths decides. Two lanes: financials and REITs use ROE (the right metric for them) and skip the ROIC/cash-cover/leverage gates.

Section 09 offers a second lens: Net income sorts survivors by net yield with a dividend-growth guard, for maximum income now.

04

Why it works, and where it is only common sense

The claim has two parts, and they have different evidential strength.

Selection is evidence, not vibes. The gates encode the one robust empirical finding in this area: dividend growers and quality compounders beat high-yielders and cutters. Ned Davis Research (S&P 500, 1973 to 2025): dividend growers returned 10.2%/yr at lower volatility; cutters returned minus 0.96%/yr. Novy-Marx (2013) on gross profitability and Asness-Frazzini-Pedersen on quality-minus-junk give the academic spine. The live ETF record agrees: quality-dividend (DGRW) 10.1%/yr real since 2013 versus yield-weighted (SDY) 6.2%.

Timing is mostly common sense plus one weak signal. The cheapness term (yield vs own history) is the only entry signal with any support, and even that is a value tilt in disguise, not alpha. We explicitly do not use RSI, chart patterns, or "wait for the dip", because those carry negative evidence for a buy-and-hold accumulator (Marshall-Cahan-Cahan 2008; Han-Yang-Zhou 2013). Waiting in cash for a better entry loses ~68-70% of the time even with perfect foresight (Maggiulli; Vanguard).

Honest bound. Dividends are not free money (Hartzmark & Solomon 2019); the snowball framing is behavioural scaffolding that keeps us buying, not a free lunch. This engine generates and disciplines. It does not certify.

05

What it cannot do

Load-bearing assumption

Cheapness cannot tell "on sale" from "in decline"

The cheapness score cannot distinguish a sound business temporarily on sale from one correctly priced for decline. A yield at 2× its own average means the price halved relative to the dividend, which is either an opportunity or a warning. Without a guardrail, cheapness alone turns the ranking into a falling-knife detector: in an early build the top three names (Capgemini, 3i, Accenture) were all down 35 to 48% over the prior year, ranked top precisely because they had crashed hardest.

The trend gate (gate 7) is the fix: it drops any name in a clear downtrend (12-month return worse than minus 10%, or sitting more than 12% below its 200-day average), so the screen no longer recommends a stock simply for collapsing. Trend is a poor signal for timing an entry (waiting for a dip loses), but a good one for excluding broken names from selection (Han-Yang-Zhou 2013).

Residual risk the gate still cannot catch: a business whose moat is eroding but whose price and numbers have not broken yet. A mild dip that passes the trend gate can still be an early-stage trap. So the screen ranks candidates, it does not vouch for them. The forward-moat call stays with you.

07

You have money this month

The rank gives an order, not a portfolio. The allocation rule, in order:

  1. Deploy the month it exists. Fixed monthly amount, in immediately. Immediate beats waiting ~68-70% of the time even with perfect hindsight.
  2. Buy the highest-ranked name that you do not already hold at target weight, and that does not push any one sector above ~25% of the sleeve.
  3. The sector cap matters here. The survivor list is heavy in financials and real estate, so naive top-down buying would over-concentrate. Cap it.
  4. Investment trusts are the one legitimate wait: buy when the discount to NAV is wider than the trust's own 5-year average (the only timing signal with a real mechanism). Yahoo does not carry NAV discounts, so check the AIC site manually.
  5. Never sell on a dip. This is accumulate-only. Re-rank monthly and let the order name your next buy.

Withholding tax & asset location

Rank on net yield, tilt toward low-tax domiciles, do not wall off the world

Foreign dividends are taxed at source and an ISA does not exempt that. For a UK investor the effective drag is roughly: UK and Hong Kong 0%, US 15% (W-8BEN treaty), Germany/France/Italy ~26%, Switzerland 35%. So a headline 5.3% Swiss yield delivers only ~3.5% net, while a 5.5% UK yield is the full 5.5%. The table now shows a Net% column (yield after withholding) and a WHT column, and the score carries a modest penalty by domicile so UK and HK names tilt up and Swiss/French/German names tilt down, in proportion.

It is a tilt, not a wall, on purpose. The UK income universe is thin and trap-prone (section 02), so going home-only would sacrifice quality and concentrate risk. The cleaner lever is asset location: US withholding falls to 0% inside a SIPP (the treaty exempts pensions), so hold the US dividend names in a SIPP and the UK and high-withholding names in the ISA. Tax should nudge the ranking, not dictate a single country.

08

Operations

  • --rerank (instant): any time you re-sort or change a threshold. Free.
  • Monthly default refresh (python3 screen.py): the real cadence. The gates move on quarterly results, so monthly is the right resolution for a monthly-buy habit. This is the one to automate.
  • --refresh after results season (late Feb, late Jul) or a sharp market drop, when fundamentals and the cheapness picture have actually moved.
  • Rebuild the universe roughly quarterly for index reconstitutions.
  • Do not re-fetch more than weekly: nothing in the gates changes intra-week and it only risks rate-limiting.

The blue-chip dividend-growth aristocrats: long, unbroken records of raising the dividend, the Coca-Cola archetype, not high-yield cash cows and not fast-growth rebounders. This screen ranks on length and quality over rate: a 25-year steady compounder beats a 7-year fast one.
